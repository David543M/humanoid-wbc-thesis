\chapter{State of the Art: Whole-Body Control for Humanoid Robots}
\label{chap:sota}

\section*{Chapter Outline}

This chapter synthesises the model-based \WBC{} literature for humanoid
robots under three framing constraints inherited from the thesis master
memory. First, the review prioritises peer-reviewed contributions from
IEEE, Springer, and Elsevier venues, supplemented by recent (post-2022)
preprints when they fill identifiable gaps. Second, the taxonomy is
preserved across the chapter: methods are grouped as task-priority /
null-space, QP-based, MPC-based, and (as baseline reference) learning-based.
Third, the chapter terminates with an explicit positioning of the proposed
thesis contribution against the canvassed body of work, in line with the
mandated logical flow.

The chapter is therefore not a chronological catalogue but an
\emph{argument}: the dominant architectural pattern (planning layer plus
execution layer) is well-established but underspecified at the coupling
interface, and the absence of a standardised evaluation protocol for
simultaneous loco-manipulation constitutes a credible target for a
master's-level contribution.

\begin{criticalinsight}
The structural choice to organise the chapter around an argument rather
than a method-by-method survey carries a methodological cost: it requires
the reader to accept the taxonomy as load-bearing before the evidence is
presented. This risk is mitigated by reserving Section~\ref{sec:sota_synthesis}
for the synthesis and by explicitly flagging, throughout, where method
boundaries blur (e.g.\ between QP-WBC and MPC at the centroidal level).
A reviewer may legitimately object that the taxonomy elides hybrid
model-learning approaches; this chapter treats those as out of scope per
the thesis scope statement, while Section~\ref{sec:sota_rl} acknowledges them
as a reference baseline.
\end{criticalinsight}


% --- 2.1 FOUNDATIONS ---------------------------------------
\section{Foundations of Whole-Body Control}
\label{sec:sota_foundations}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\input{figures/fig_wbc_taxonomy}}
  \caption{Taxonomy of Whole-Body Control (WBC) approaches for humanoid robots.
           Model-based methods (blue) are the primary focus of this chapter;
           learning-based approaches (grey) are retained as baseline references only
           (see Section~\ref{sec:sota_rl}).
           Representative works are indicated in brackets.}
  \label{fig:wbc_taxonomy}
\end{figure}

\WBC{} emerged at the intersection of operational-space control
\cite{Khatib1987} and floating-base rigid-body dynamics, motivated by the
recognition that a humanoid robot's degrees of freedom cannot be
commanded independently without violating either dynamic feasibility or
contact-stability constraints \cite{Sentis2005}. The defining problem WBC
addresses is the \emph{simultaneous coordination of a high-DOF system}
(typically $28$--$32$ DOF for humanoids: torso, arms, and legs) under a
hierarchy of behavioural objectives, while respecting the physical
constraints imposed by joint limits, actuator torque saturation, and
unilateral contact wrenches \cite{Sentis2005,Wensing2013}.

The canonical formulation considers a humanoid as a \emph{floating-base}
system: six unactuated degrees of freedom describe the base pose relative
to an inertial frame, augmented by $n$ actuated joint variables. The
governing equation of motion takes the standard form
\begin{equation}
  \M\,\ddq + \Cg = \bm{S}\transpose\,\torque + \Jc\transpose\,\fc
  \label{eq:eom_floating_base}
\end{equation}
where $\M$ denotes the mass matrix, $\Cg$ collects the Coriolis,
centrifugal, and gravity terms, $\bm{S}$ is the actuation selection
matrix, $\torque$ collects the actuated joint torques, and
$(\Jc,\fc)$ are the contact Jacobian and the stacked contact wrench
respectively \cite{Sentis2005,Wensing2013,Koolen2016}. WBC frameworks
differ primarily in \emph{how} they reconcile multiple, potentially
conflicting task objectives over \eqref{eq:eom_floating_base} while
ensuring that $\fc$ lies inside the friction cone.

Three foundational design choices have shaped the field. The first is
the choice of \emph{control level}: kinematic (velocity),
inverse-dynamic (acceleration), or torque. Torque-level WBC, although
computationally heavier, is generally regarded as the most expressive
level for handling contact-rich behaviours \cite{Wensing2013}. The
second is the choice of \emph{task representation}: end-effector
position, momentum, centroidal quantities, or arbitrary task-space
coordinates \cite{Koolen2016}. The third is \emph{how priorities
between tasks are enforced} --- through null-space projection
(Section~\ref{sec:sota_nullspace}), through weighted optimisation
(Section~\ref{sec:sota_qp}), or through hierarchical optimisation with
strict priorities in Hierarchical \QP{} form
\cite{Sentis2005,Wensing2013,Koolen2016,EscandeMansard2014}.

\begin{criticalinsight}
The foundational WBC formulation already exposes the central trade-off
that recurs throughout the literature: \emph{constraint satisfaction}
versus \emph{computational tractability}. Torque-level WBC with hard
friction-cone constraints is mathematically rigorous but compute-intensive;
kinematic-level WBC with weighted priorities is fast but elides the
contact physics. The thesis's choice to operate at QP-torque level inherits
the former trade-off and must justify the additional cost in
Chapter~\ref{chap:sota}'s successor: a justification that rests on the
rigorous constraint handling that later sections will document.
\end{criticalinsight}


% --- 2.2 TASK-PRIORITY AND NULL-SPACE METHODS --------------
\section{Task-Priority and Null-Space Methods}
\label{sec:sota_nullspace}

The earliest \WBC{} formulation that generalised cleanly to floating-base
humanoids is the task-priority null-space approach introduced by
Sentis and Khatib \cite{Sentis2005}. Their hierarchical control of
behavioural primitives expresses each task as a desired acceleration in
a chosen task space; lower-priority tasks are projected into the null
space of the higher-priority Jacobians $\J$, ensuring that they are
executed \emph{only insofar as they do not perturb} higher-priority
objectives. This formulation has the appealing property of \emph{strict}
hierarchy: the priority ordering is enforced exactly, not approximately.

Subsequent work refined this framework along three axes. The first axis
is \emph{smoothness at task transitions}, where pseudo-inverse switching
can produce velocity discontinuities --- addressed by continuous
null-space projectors \cite{Sentis2005}. The second is the \emph{handling
of singularities}, where Jacobian rank deficiencies require regularisation
or task reactivation strategies. The third is the \emph{integration of
inequality constraints}: classical null-space methods natively handle
equality constraints (e.g.\ desired task accelerations) but offer no
clean mechanism for inequality constraints such as friction cones, joint
limits, or torque bounds \cite{Wensing2013}.

Empirically, null-space WBC has been demonstrated on humanoid systems
including the original DLR-Justin and a range of simulated platforms,
achieving task hierarchies of moderate depth (typically three to five
priority levels) \cite{Sentis2005}. Its computational cost is
favourable --- projector chains can be evaluated in milliseconds for
high-DOF systems --- but the absence of an inequality-constraint
mechanism is broadly recognised as a fundamental limitation when contact
stability or torque limits become binding \cite{Wensing2013,Koolen2016}.

\begin{criticalinsight}
Null-space WBC is best understood as an \emph{expressive but partial}
solution: it answers the question ``how do I order tasks?'' rigorously,
but cannot answer ``how do I respect physical bounds while doing so?''
without auxiliary mechanisms. The QP-based reformulation discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:sota_qp} emerged precisely to absorb both questions into
a single optimisation problem. A reviewer might argue, fairly, that
null-space methods are not obsolete --- they remain attractive when
constraints are slack and compute is at a premium --- but the debate is
context-dependent and unresolved. The thesis takes a principled position
toward QP-WBC while refraining from dismissing null-space methods
uncritically.
\end{criticalinsight}


% --- 2.3 QP-BASED WHOLE-BODY CONTROL -----------------------
\section{QP-based Whole-Body Control Formulations}
\label{sec:sota_qp}

The \QP{} formulation reframes the \WBC{} problem as a constrained
optimisation solved at every control cycle \cite{Wensing2013}. Decision
variables typically include joint accelerations $\ddq$, contact wrenches
$\fc$, and (optionally) torques $\torque$; the cost function is a
weighted sum of task tracking errors, commonly expressed through norms
$\norm{\bm{e}}$ over task-space error signals; and the constraints
encode the rigid-body dynamics \eqref{eq:eom_floating_base}, joint and
torque limits, and contact-friction conditions
\cite{Wensing2013,Koolen2016}. The first contribution to formalise this
approach with simultaneous satisfaction of ZMP and friction constraints
at all priority levels was Wensing and Orin's 2013 paper on conic
optimisation for dynamic humanoid behaviours \cite{Wensing2013}, which
demonstrated dynamic kicks and jumps on a 26-DOF simulated humanoid with
a roughly 2$\times$ computational speed-up over prior task-space methods.

Three subsequent lines of work consolidated QP-WBC as the model-based
standard. First, \emph{momentum-based formulations} \cite{Koolen2016}
reframed the cost around centroidal momentum tracking and were deployed
on the Atlas humanoid at the DARPA Robotics Challenge, with foot-placement
errors reported up to $5$~cm during rough-terrain walking \cite{Koolen2016}.
Second, \emph{Hierarchical QP (HQP)} formulations introduced strict
priorities into the QP cost, generalising the null-space approach of
Section~\ref{sec:sota_nullspace} to inequality-constrained settings
\cite{EscandeMansard2014}. Third, whole-body admittance control with
\QP{} wrench distribution extended QP-WBC to industrial multi-contact
tasks such as stair climbing on the HRP-4 platform \cite{Caron2019}.

Recent post-2022 work has sharpened the focus on \emph{computational
robustness and real-time contact discovery}. Contact-implicit \MPC{} formulations
\cite{LeCleach2024,Kim2025CI} eliminate the need for pre-defined contact
mode sequences by solving directly over contact decision variables, enabling
real-time multi-contact discovery. Smaldone et al.\
\cite{Smaldone2025} show that explicit attention to the compute budget
at the planner/WBC interface yields robust dynamic walking. Across these
works, QP solve times remain in the \emph{sub-millisecond regime} when
implemented with tuned solvers such as Eiquadprog or PROXQP \cite{DelPrete2016,Wensing2023Review}.

A key methodological observation is that \emph{friction-cone compliance}
in QP-WBC is rarely reported as a percentage in the literature, because
the QP enforces it as a hard inequality constraint: violations correspond
to solver infeasibility events, not to gradual non-compliance. This
observation is revisited in Section~\ref{sec:sota_synthesis}.

\begin{criticalinsight}
QP-WBC is the field's mature technical answer to the multi-task,
multi-constraint problem, but its maturity hides a measurement problem:
most papers report constraint \emph{satisfaction} qualitatively (the QP
was feasible) rather than quantitatively (what fraction of cycles, under
what perturbation budget). The post-2022 literature has begun to expose
this gap \cite{LeCleach2024,Smaldone2025} but has not yet converged on a
reporting protocol --- a gap Section~\ref{sec:sota_synthesis} will argue
the thesis can credibly target. A second insight is that the boundary
between QP-WBC and MPC is increasingly porous: at the centroidal level,
the same dynamics is being optimised in both, just over different
horizons. This blurring is the subject of Section~\ref{sec:sota_mpc}.
\end{criticalinsight}

% --- 2.4 MODEL PREDICTIVE CONTROL --------------------------
\section{Model Predictive Control for Humanoid Locomotion}
\label{sec:sota_mpc}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \input{figures/fig_wbc_architecture}
  \caption{Hierarchical planning/execution architecture for model-based humanoid
           Whole-Body Control. The centroidal MPC layer generates wrench references
           and a contact schedule at 50--100\,Hz; the QP-based WBC layer distributes
           these to joint torques at 1\,kHz. The coupling interface (orange) is the
           underdocumented boundary identified as a methodological gap in the
           literature and constitutes a primary contribution of this thesis.}
  \label{fig:wbc_architecture}
\end{figure}

\MPC{} addresses a complementary problem to \QP-based \WBC: instead of
solving an instantaneous tracking optimisation at each cycle, MPC solves
a finite-horizon optimal control problem over a predicted trajectory
\cite{Wensing2023Review}. For humanoid locomotion, MPC is dominantly
applied at the \emph{centroidal} level --- optimising \com{} and
angular-momentum trajectories under contact constraints
\cite{Caron2019,Romualdi2023,Elobaid2023} --- because full-body MPC at
high frequency is computationally intractable.

Three architectures dominate the post-2022 literature. The first is the
\emph{linear / linearised centroidal MPC} lineage descended from the
Linear Inverted Pendulum Model, which preserves real-time feasibility
(under $10$~ms per solve) at the cost of accuracy in highly dynamic
regimes \cite{Smaldone2022}. The second is \emph{non-linear centroidal MPC},
exemplified by Romualdi et al.\ \cite{Romualdi2023} and Elobaid et al.\
\cite{Elobaid2023}, which retains full centroidal non-linearity at the
cost of solve times in the $50$--$70$~ms range on the iCub3 platform
\cite{Elobaid2023}. The third is \emph{trajectory optimisation with
phase-based parameterisation} \cite{Winkler2018}, which absorbs gait
sequencing into the optimisation itself by treating step timings and
footholds as decision variables --- a structural innovation subsequently
adopted in step-adjustment-aware MPC \cite{Romualdi2023}.

A separate trajectory-optimisation tradition uses \emph{multi-shooting}
and DDP-class algorithms over the full-body dynamics. Crocoddyl
\cite{Crocoddyl} is the reference implementation, supporting
Feasibility-driven Differential Dynamic Programming (FDDP) over
multi-contact problems and computing dynamic manoeuvres (jumps,
front-flips) within milliseconds. Crocoddyl is built on Pinocchio's
analytical-derivative dynamics \cite{Pinocchio}, which evaluates
legged-robot dynamics in approximately $3~\mu$s and is the de facto
industry standard.

The interface between MPC (planning layer) and QP-WBC (execution layer)
is the \emph{unresolved} element of this literature. The post-2022 sweep
confirms it remains open: Sleiman et al.\ \cite{Sleiman2021}, Romualdi et al.\
\cite{Romualdi2023}, and Smaldone et al.\ \cite{Smaldone2025} each propose
plausible coupling architectures using mutually incompatible terminology.

\begin{criticalinsight}
MPC and QP-WBC are not competing methods but layered ones, and the more
useful question is not ``MPC or WBC?'' but ``what is the right interface
between them?'' The current literature offers at least three answers ---
sequential time-scale separation \cite{Romualdi2023}, unified multi-contact
optimal control \cite{Sleiman2021}, and contact-implicit \MPC{} with
automatic mode discovery \cite{LeCleach2024} --- but no consensus. This is the precise methodological
territory the thesis enters in its proposed methodology chapter;
Section~\ref{sec:sota_synthesis} will argue that \emph{naming and unifying}
this design space, even before innovating within it, is a defensible
Chapter~\ref{chap:sota} contribution.
\end{criticalinsight}


% --- 2.5 LEARNING-BASED APPROACHES (BASELINE) --------------
\section{Learning-based Approaches}
\label{sec:sota_rl}

Reinforcement Learning (RL) approaches to humanoid \WBC{} have advanced
rapidly since 2022 but are treated in this thesis as a \emph{baseline for
comparison} rather than a contribution axis. This positioning is required
by the thesis scope but also aligns with three substantive observations
from the literature.

First, RL-based WBC has demonstrated impressive \emph{agility}:
end-to-end learned policies on Cassie and Unitree H1 achieve dynamic
walking, recovery from large perturbations, and sim-to-real transfer
with domain randomisation
\cite{Radosavovic2024,HumanoidGym2024,PolySim2025}. Second, however, RL
approaches generally \emph{forfeit interpretability and constraint
guarantees}: they cannot enforce hard friction-cone or torque-saturation
constraints by construction, only encourage compliance through reward
shaping. Third, the evaluation reporting in the RL literature is markedly
more quantitative than in the model-based literature: HumanoidBench
\cite{Sferrazza2024} reports 27-task success rates on Unitree H1, and
PolySim reports a $+52.8\%$ sim-to-MuJoCo motion-tracking improvement
under multi-simulator domain randomisation \cite{PolySim2025}.

A growing class of \emph{hybrid model-learning} approaches ---
contact-implicit \MPC{} with learned warm-starting \cite{LeCleach2024}
and real-world RL-based loco-manipulation \cite{Radosavovic2024} --- sits between
the two camps. These works are outside the thesis scope but are noted
here as evidence that the model-based / learning-based distinction is
itself becoming porous.

The thesis adopts a pre-trained RL policy (e.g.\ for Unitree H1
locomotion) as an \emph{upper-bound reference} baseline, against which
the proposed QP-WBC framework is compared on the metrics defined in the
thesis's benchmark-metrics specification.

\begin{criticalinsight}
The decision to scope RL out of the contribution but in to the baselines
is methodologically coherent but epistemically uncomfortable: if RL
outperforms the proposed WBC on every metric, the thesis must defend its
design goals (constraint guarantees, interpretability) explicitly rather
than performance-numerically. The response --- \emph{RL trades constraints
for agility, different design goals} --- is sustainable but must be
reinforced by demonstrating a \emph{qualitative} advantage of the WBC
framework, not merely a quantitative one.
Section~\ref{sec:sota_synthesis} returns to this point.
\end{criticalinsight}


% --- 2.6 SIMULATION ENVIRONMENTS AND TOOLCHAINS ------------
\section{Simulation Environments and Toolchains}
\label{sec:sota_sim}

Simulation choice is non-incidental for a simulation-only thesis: it
determines what physical behaviours are reproducible and what claims are
defensible. The literature converges on four primary platforms.

\emph{Pinocchio} \cite{Pinocchio} is the dominant rigid-body dynamics
library: it provides RBDL-class forward and inverse dynamics with
analytical derivatives, evaluating in approximately $3~\mu$s per call on
legged systems. It is the backbone of Stack-of-Tasks and Crocoddyl
\cite{Crocoddyl} and is the de facto standard for \QP-\WBC{}
implementations.

\emph{Crocoddyl} \cite{Crocoddyl} extends Pinocchio for trajectory
optimisation under contact sequences, with Feasibility-driven DDP as its
solver innovation. Crocoddyl is positioned as the reference toolchain
for full-body MPC.

\emph{MuJoCo} \cite{MuJoCo2012} is widely used for contact-rich humanoid
Simulation, valued for its stable spring-damper soft-contact model and
computational speed. Its principal limitation is that soft contacts
diverge from rigid hardware reality in ways that are platform-dependent.

\emph{Isaac Sim} (NVIDIA) provides GPU-accelerated, photorealistic
Simulation with rigid-body dynamics, enabling massively parallel RL
training \cite{HumanoidGym2024}. Its model-based use for WBC remains
less documented but is growing \cite{PolySim2025}.

A recent benchmarking effort by Fernbach et al.\ \cite{Fernbach2018}
explicitly compared three control schemes (HQP velocity, TSID
acceleration, TSID torque) on TALOS in Gazebo, reporting \com{} tracking
peaks of $5$~cm in $x$ and $1.5$~cm in $y$ during walking. This benchmark
is the closest existing analogue to the evaluation protocol the thesis
proposes and is therefore a critical reference point for the methodology
and experiments chapters.

\begin{criticalinsight}
The toolchain landscape is not neutral. Pinocchio\,+\,MuJoCo commits to a
soft-contact model whose divergence from rigid hardware is platform-dependent
\cite{MuJoCo2012}, while Pinocchio\,+\,Isaac Sim commits to a GPU-determinism
assumption that complicates reproducibility on CPU-bound platforms. The
literature consistently positions MuJoCo as the preferred environment for
torque-controlled, model-based \WBC{} research
\cite{DelPrete2016,Koolen2016,MuJoCo2012}, whereas Isaac Sim dominates
data-driven RL workflows \cite{HumanoidGym2024,PolySim2025}. This partition
reflects a deeper difference in evaluation goals --- constraint satisfaction
guarantees vs.\ sample-efficient policy learning --- and directly motivates
the contact-model justification in \Cref{sec:contact-modeling} and the
simulation setup in \Cref{chap:simulation}.
\end{criticalinsight}


% --- 2.7 CRITICAL SYNTHESIS --- GAPS AND POSITIONING -------
\section{Critical Synthesis --- Gaps and Positioning}
\label{sec:sota_synthesis}

The preceding sections establish three structural facts about the
model-based humanoid \WBC{} literature.

The first is that the dominant architectural pattern is \emph{layered}:
a planning layer --- centroidal \MPC{}, footstep planner, or trajectory
optimisation --- supplies references to an execution layer built around
a \QP-based WBC at torque level, and this split is widely acknowledged
as the pragmatic resolution of the centroidal-versus-full-body dynamics
controversy \cite{Wensing2013,Koolen2016,Caron2019,Romualdi2023,Elobaid2023}.
Post-2022 contributions --- whether focused on contact-implicit planning
\cite{LeCleach2024,Kim2025CI}, computationally
efficient footstep planners coupled to WBC \cite{Smaldone2025}, or
contact-stable force parametrisations under payload \cite{Elobaid2023}
--- all retain this two-layer architecture. The thesis architecture
(centroidal MPC paired with QP-WBC) therefore inherits a consolidated
design pattern rather than proposing a new one.

The second fact is that, despite this architectural consolidation, the
\emph{interface between the two layers remains underspecified}.
Romualdi et al.\ couple the layers via non-linear centroidal MPC with
step timing as an optimisation variable \cite{Romualdi2023}; Elobaid
et al.\ extend this coupling with a contact-stable force parametrisation
for payload carrying \cite{Elobaid2023}; Sleiman et al.\ unify the
whole-body loco-manipulation problem as a single multi-contact optimal
control problem \cite{Sleiman2021}; Smaldone et al.\ propose
compute-budget-aware decoupling at the planner/WBC boundary
\cite{Smaldone2025}; Le Cleac'h et al.\ eliminate the need for
pre-defined contact sequences through contact-implicit \MPC{}
\cite{LeCleach2024}. These proposals are plausible in isolation but use
incompatible terminology, and no published work surveyed here reconciles
them into a common formalism or compares them on a shared benchmark.

The third fact is that the \emph{evaluation protocol for simultaneous
loco-manipulation is effectively absent}. The unified MPC formulation of
Sleiman et al.\ for whole-body dynamic locomotion and manipulation
\cite{Sleiman2021} is a rare case in which the simultaneous
loco-manipulation scenario is exercised under model-based control;
recent learning-based loco-manipulation works
\cite{Radosavovic2024,Sferrazza2024} sidestep the constraint-enforcement
question entirely. Friction-cone compliance is rarely reported
quantitatively because QP-WBC enforces it as a hard constraint by
construction \cite{Wensing2013,Koolen2016}, and task success rates are
almost never accompanied by confidence intervals or infeasibility
statistics even in the TALOS benchmarking study of Fernbach et al.\
\cite{Fernbach2018}, which is otherwise the closest analogue to a
standardised evaluation.

These three facts resolve into three explicit gaps that define the space
available to the thesis.

\begin{researchgap}
\textbf{Methodological gap --- planner/WBC coupling.} No consensus
framework couples a planning layer to a \QP-\WBC{} execution layer
across different time scales, even as several candidate couplings
coexist in the literature without a reconciliation mechanism
\cite{Romualdi2023,Sleiman2021,Smaldone2025,LeCleach2024}.
\end{researchgap}

\begin{researchgap}
\textbf{Evaluation gap --- loco-manipulation protocol.} No standardised
scenario set, metric set, or reporting convention governs simultaneous
humanoid loco-manipulation under \WBC{}. The existing TALOS benchmark
restricts itself to locomotion-only tasks \cite{Fernbach2018}, and
published whole-body loco-manipulation studies do not share a common
evaluation template \cite{Sleiman2021}.
\end{researchgap}

\begin{researchgap}
\textbf{Measurement gap --- QP infeasibility as silent failure.} The
model-based community treats QP infeasibility as a silent failure mode
rather than a first-class observable, even though every infeasibility
event is informative given the hard-constraint formulation
\cite{Wensing2013,Koolen2016}. No widely adopted reporting protocol
tracks infeasibility rates, contact-cycle feasibility, or
confidence-intervaled task success.
\end{researchgap}

Against these three gaps the thesis positions its contribution with
deliberate modesty in one direction and deliberate ambition in the
other. The thesis does \emph{not} claim radical algorithmic novelty:
the planning layer uses established non-linear centroidal MPC
formulations \cite{Romualdi2023,Elobaid2023}, the execution layer uses a
standard weighted QP-WBC \cite{Wensing2013,Koolen2016}, and the
dynamics and optimisation stack reuses mature open-source infrastructure
\cite{Pinocchio,Crocoddyl}. A reviewer expecting a new solver, a new
cost formulation, or a new contact model will not find one in the
methodology chapter. What the thesis does claim is a
\emph{consolidation-and-validation} contribution, which can be stated
along three concrete axes. First, one explicit coupling between
centroidal MPC and QP-WBC is instantiated in Simulation and documented
at a level of detail sufficient for reproduction, closing part of the
methodological gap. Second, evaluation is performed on a scenario set
$S_1$--$S_5$ that includes the simultaneous loco-manipulation case most
often omitted in the literature \cite{Sleiman2021,Fernbach2018}. Third,
the reporting protocol treats QP infeasibility rates, contact-cycle
feasibility, and confidence-intervaled task success as first-class
metrics. Taken individually, each of these pieces exists somewhere in
the literature; taken together, under rigorous reporting, they do not.
This conjunction is what qualifies the thesis as a master's-level
contribution with plausible publication value.

\begin{criticalinsight}
Chapter~\ref{chap:sota} itself has two acknowledged limits that should
temper the strength of its claims. The first is that its evidentiary
base is bounded by the seven verified foundational sources and the
twenty-five post-2022 candidates triaged during preparation; papers not
retrieved by the targeted search --- in particular non-English venues
and robotics workshops not indexed by standard paper corpora --- may
contain counter-examples to the evaluation-absence claim, and the
strength of the third gap above is therefore contingent on the
completeness of the sweep rather than on an exhaustive proof of
non-existence. The second limit is that hybrid model-plus-learning
approaches \cite{LeCleach2024,Radosavovic2024} are deliberately scoped
out, even though their coupling-of-layers strategies are methodologically
closest to what the methodology chapter will propose. This exclusion is
defensible given the thesis scope, but it leaves open the reviewer
question of whether a learning-augmented MPC would subsume the proposed
contribution; that question is deferred to the discussion chapter
rather than answered here.
\end{criticalinsight}
